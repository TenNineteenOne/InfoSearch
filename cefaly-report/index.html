02

Mechanism of Action

CEFALY targets the trigeminal nerve — the primary nociceptive pathway in migraine — via a supraorbital bipolar self-adhesive electrode placed across the forehead. The device delivers rectangular biphasic symmetrical pulses at 100 Hz (verum), with current intensity ramping linearly to a maximum of 16 mA over the first 14 minutes, then held constant.

Two operational modes correspond to distinct physiological targets:

PREVENT Mode (20 min/day)
Modulates trigeminovascular sensitization over time; raises the migraine trigger threshold with repeated daily stimulation. Effect is cumulative — full benefit typically emerges over 4–12 weeks.
ACUTE Mode (60–120 min)
Targets active nociceptive signaling during an attack. Applied at earliest sign of migraine onset; works via inhibition of trigeminal nucleus caudalis afferent transmission.
03

Clinical Evidence Summary

Three pivotal sham-controlled RCTs form the core evidence base. ⚠ All three were sponsored or co-designed by CEFALY Technology — an important funding caveat noted throughout. Independent replication is lacking.

PREMICE (2013) Prevention Schoenen et al. · Neurology 2013

Prospective, multicenter, double-blind, randomized, sham-controlled trial across 5 Belgian tertiary headache clinics. After a 1-month run-in, patients with ≥2 migraine attacks/month were randomized to verum or sham (20 min/day × 3 months). N=67 ITT.

Primary outcomes: change in monthly migraine days (MMDs) and 50% responder rate.

N=67 ITT 38.2% responder rate vs 12.1% sham p=0.023 30% MMD reduction ⚠ Industry-sponsored ⚠ Underpowered
ACME (2018) Acute — 1hr Chou et al. · Cephalalgia 2019

Double-blind, randomized, sham-controlled study across 3 U.S. headache centers. Adults experiencing an acute migraine attack (with or without aura) randomized 1:1 to verum or sham eTNS for 1 hour. N=106 ITT (verum n=52; sham n=54).

Primary outcome: mean change in pain intensity via VAS at 1 hour.

N=106 ITT 79% pain relief 32% pain freedom 59% pain intensity reduction Effect sustained 24hrs ⚠ Industry-sponsored
TEAM (2022) Acute — 2hr · Phase 3 Kuruvilla et al. · Scientific Reports 2022

Prospective, multicenter, randomized, double-blind, sham-controlled Phase 3 trial. N=538 adults (ICHD-3 criteria, 2–8 migraine headache days/month) randomized 1:1 to 2-hour active or sham for a single moderate-to-severe attack treated at home. Largest sham-controlled trial of any eTNS therapy for migraine.

Primary endpoints: pain freedom at 2hrs and freedom from most bothersome symptom (MBS) at 2hrs.

N=538 — largest eTNS RCT 26% pain freedom (vs 16% sham) 58.9% MBS freedom (vs 42.2% sham) p=0.0167 pain freedom p=0.0006 MBS freedom ⚠ Industry-sponsored

05

Safety & Adverse Effects

AEs were reported in 7.05% of active stimulation participants and 2.64% of sham participants in the TEAM study. Most common: forehead paresthesia, discomfort, or burning (3.5% active group). No serious adverse events in any pivotal trial.

Common
  • Post-session somnolence
  • Headache after a session
Uncommon
  • Forehead skin erythema
  • Nausea
  • Forehead paresthesia / burning
Rare
  • Forehead contact allergy (acrylate)
⚠️

Black Box Warnings: None.  |  Contraindications: Implanted cardiac devices (pacemakers, loop monitors, ICDs) — contraindicated; risk of interference unknown per FDA labeling. Active implanted neurostimulators. Known electrode material allergy. Do not use during driving or operating machinery — device induces somnolence during and within 1 hour of treatment. Not studied in pregnancy; voluntary pregnancy registry active.

07

Evidence Gaps & Limitations

⚠️

Small PREMICE trial (N=67): Substantially underpowered relative to modern regulatory standards; limits generalizability of all prevention efficacy data.

⚠️

All major trials industry-sponsored: CEFALY Technology participated in design, data collection, analysis, and manuscript writing across all three pivotal studies. Independent replication is lacking.

⚠️

No direct head-to-head trial vs. active pharmacological comparator (triptans, topiramate, CGRP mAbs): All efficacy benchmarks above are indirect or network meta-analytic comparisons.

⚠️

Chronic migraine population: FDA indication is episodic migraine only. A single-center, open-label pilot explored eTNS in chronic migraine, but no large sham-controlled trial has been completed in this population.

⚠️

TEAM therapeutic gain (9.55%): While statistically significant, the absolute gain over sham is modest; clinicians should contextualize this carefully in shared decision-making conversations.

⚠️

Long-term durability data: No trials beyond 3–6 months follow-up; durability of preventive effect after cessation is unknown.

⚠️

Sham-blinding integrity: Device trials are inherently harder to blind than drug RCTs — patients feeling tingling from verum but nothing from sham may correctly identify their group, potentially inflating apparent treatment effect.

⚠️

Pediatric population: Not studied; FDA indication limited to adults ≥18 years of age.

Bottom Line

CEFALY has credible, peer-reviewed RCT evidence supporting modest but statistically significant efficacy for both acute and preventive episodic migraine treatment, with an excellent safety profile; however, all pivotal trials are industry-sponsored, the prevention trial is substantially underpowered, therapeutic gain over sham is modest for acute use, and no head-to-head pharmacological comparator data exist — positioning it best as an adjunct or a preferred option for patients with contraindications to, intolerance of, or preference against pharmacological therapies.

Access Summary

Medicare non-coverage (A9270) and inconsistent commercial payer DME reimbursement are the primary access barriers; out-of-pocket cost is approximately $500 upfront plus $350–$450/year in electrodes, which over 2–3 years is cost-competitive with branded pharmacological prevention options.
